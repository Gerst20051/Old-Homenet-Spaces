Google promises 210 jobs in Lenoir

Internet search engine giant Google Inc. announced Friday plans to establish a facility in western North Carolina that will employ more than 200 workers.

Google said it will spend as much as $600 million to build a data center in the Caldwell County town of Lenoir. The North Carolina operations will create 210 new jobs, which will have an average annual salary of $48,300.

In exchange, Google stands to gain a state and local incentives package that would rank among the largest in North Carolina economic development history.

The state has granted Google a performance-based Jobs Development Investment Grant worth as much as $4.8 million over 12 years. In addition, the company could save as much as $20 million under the state's "Tax Credits for Growing Businesses tax credit program, also referred to as Article 3J tax credits, according to Department of Commerce spokeswoman Deborah Barnes. The Article 3J system replaced what was formerly known as the William S. Lee tax credit system.

The amount that Google stands to gain from state tax credits is dependent upon the amount of money the company pays each year in taxes.

Google also will benefit from concessions made by Caldwell County. Officials say the company has been granted a 100 percent waiver on its business property taxes over the next 30 years, as well as an 80 percent waiver on real-estate taxes over the same period.

Telephone calls seeking comment from Caldwell County officials were not immediately returned.

To date, the largest state and local incentives package totaled $242 million to Dell in 2000 in exchange for operations slated to employ 1,500 workers.

Google Inc. plans $600 million data center in N.C.

Search engine giant Google Inc. plans to spend $600 million to build a data center in North Carolina, state officials and the company said Friday.

The so-called "server farm" will eventually employ 210 people in a region hit hard in recent years by layoffs in the furniture and textile industries.

"We're absolutely thrilled to death for our community and our citizens," said Lenoir Mayor David Barlow. The center will be built in his city of 60,000 about 60 miles northwest of Charlotte.

"This company will provide hundreds of good-paying, knowledge-based jobs that North Carolina's citizens want," Gov. Mike Easley said in a statement. "It will help reinvigorate an area hard hit by the loss of furniture and textile jobs with 21st century opportunities."

The state will give the company $4.8 million as part of a total incentives package that could reach more than $100 million.

"The data center we're planning to build ... will help support continued growth in our online services," said Lloyd Taylor, director of global operations for Mountain View, Calif.-based Google.

The average annual salary at the data center is expected to be $48,300, about $20,000 more than the average salary in Caldwell County.

Tim Sanders, a former Caldwell County commissioner who helped lead negotiations with the company, said the site for the center in Lenoir already had part of the infrastructure in place.

Officials hope the Google data center will help bring more high-tech companies to the area.

"We still have good furniture jobs here and we hope to continue to grow those, but we're also diversifying in high tech, Sanders said.

The recruitment of Google is the second high-tech score for the state's economic developers since 2004, when both the state and local governments gave Dell Inc. $280 million worth of incentives to build a computer plant in Winston-Salem. The Dell plant opened in October 2005.

Welcome to the future.

A new era dawned for Caldwell County Thursday with the announcement by global Internet leader Google that it plans to build a large-scale computer data center in Lenoir.

In confirming the development plans, officials from Google braved a bit of winter weather to meet with a host of local business, civic and academic leaders. Google's selection of Lenoir comes after being wooed for the past 13 months, which included countless meetings, top-secret negotiations and unprecedented economic incentives.

“It really wasn't the incentives, per se,” Google Senior Leader Rhett Weiss said. “Lenoir had some fine infrastructure installed at one point for the furniture industry.

“The city, county and the EDC (Caldwell County Economic Development Commission) had a fine team to work with us on the project.”

According to Google's Director of Global Operations Lloyd Taylor, the company plans initially to construct a single, one-story building to house its massive banks of computer servers. He said there is the possibility of adding additional buildings to the campus in the future.
 

 “It would depend on demand for our services,” Taylor said. “It's an open question for us. Weiss said Google has taken ownership of more than 220 acres of property located in the Overlook Drive and Virginia Street area previously procured by the Caldwell County EDC, LLC. The company also will maintain long-term options to purchase additional land behind the Bernhardt Furniture Company headquarters. Google also granted an option back to Duke Energy in the development area if there is a need to provide additional power capacity, Weiss said.

Grading in the project area has been ongoing since early October, following several months of property acquisition dating back to June. Taylor said he anticipates construction to begin in a few months, employing 200 to 400 people during the 12- to 18-month building phase.
“I don't think I've ever built a data center that turned out exactly how I thought it would.” We'll start with one and see what the future holds.

Taylor emphasized the company's intention to hire local residents once the site becomes operational. He said the company likely will hire 100 people initially, including 10 to 20 systems analysts and 50 to 75 hardware technicians. Google will seek college-educated workers for the systems analyst jobs, and hopes to retrain skilled laborers that had worked in local factories for other positions, Taylor said.

A separate workforce level will involve subcontracted support staff, including security, maintenance, grounds keeping and catering. Many of Google's incentives are tied to an investment goal of $600 million and the creation of 210 jobs.

“Our goal is to hire locally,” Taylor said. “It's just good business.”

Taylor, Weiss and East Coast Regional Manager Andy Johnson visited Caldwell Community College and Technical Institute (CCC&TI) as part of the day's activities. Johnson, who visited the planned construction site for the first time Thursday, is expected to oversee development operations.

“You are coming at a most opportune time,” CCC&TI President Ken Boham said. “There is a workforce here that is ready to go to work.

“Furniture has been our heritage, but education is our future.”

Appalachian State University Chancellor Ken Peacock offered his support in gearing academic programs for Google.

“We are here to find out how we can meet (Google's) needs,” Peacock said. “We look forward in learning how Appalachian State can work with Caldwell (Community College) to meet your expectations.”

The two colleges recently culminated a partnership last year that resulted in Appalachian State University courses being offered on the CCC&TI campus in Hudson. Having both institutions available for training opportunities will be important to Google.

Taylor said when he worked at a previous technology company he helped the University of Maryland develop a four-year degree program focused on computer information systems.

“It's not a matter of how smart you are, it's what you can do,” Taylor said. “A lot of what Google is about is enabling. We want to enable people to do what they want to do.”

Google's entry is a major boost for a county that has been devastated economically by the loss of furniture jobs. The unemployment rate, after soaring above 13 percent in 2005, still remains the second highest in the state at 8.4 percent through November.

In landing Google, Caldwell County and the City of Lenoir granted the company a 100 percent waiver of business property taxes and an 80 percent waiver of real estate property taxes over the next 30 years. In addition, Google has been awarded a Jobs Development Incentives Grant valued at up to $4.77 million from the North Carolina Department of Commerce.
