Teenage Resourcefulness

Teenagers need to practice being resourceful if they are to survive in the ever-changing adult world. Giving advice based on our own experience is all too easy for most parents but consider the following:

  • How will your teen cope in a crisis if you're not there?
  • Is the best solution for you the best solution for your teen?

As teens grow older, the likelihood of you, the parent, always being on hand when things go wrong becomes less. Teens start to go out more and more with their friends rather than their family. Temptations are put in their way and your teen has to decide how to deal with, there and then.

As a parent you need to feel confident that your teen will know how to deal with situations as and when they happen. More importantly, your teen needs to know how to handle situations confidently and safely. Supporting your teen is all about helping them find their own solution based on their skills not yours.


 

So what needs to change?

Teens need to be able to come up with their own solutions. Asking your teen to come up with ideas and possible solutions gets your teen thinking. Getting them to then look at the pros and cons of each option furthers their decision-making skills. In particular, it helps your teen to start thinking about risk and making judgments.

Understanding their own strengths and weaknesses is important for the teen. If the teen over-estimates their ability to cope, it could lead to a greater problem with painful consequences. If the teen underestimates their own ability, it could lead to them being overly dependent on others.

Consequently, teens need to have feedback not only verbally from parents, teachers, etc on their strengths, but also from experiencing the consequences of their actions. In other words, they also need to take responsibility for their solutions.

Allowing teens to practice this process in increasingly complex, but safe, situations will have them well prepared for when the unexpected does happen and you are not around to support them. ‘You-centred’ listening will help both you and your teen determine exactly how you go about this.
