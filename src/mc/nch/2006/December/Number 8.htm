Shuttle Discovery blasts off from Cape Canaveral

M&C Science

The space shuttle Discovery lifts off from Launch Pad 39B at the Kennedy Space Center in Cape Canaveral, Florida, December 9, 2006. REUTERS/David Carlson

Washington - The Space Shuttle Discovery lifted off late Saturday from the Kennedy Space Centre at Cape Canaveral, Florida.

It was the first night shuttle launch by the US space agency NASA in four years.

The twin solid-fuel rocket boosters separated successfully about three minutes into the flight. With Discovery a bright dot in the sky over Cape Canaveral, the two reusable booster rockets floated away from the shuttle in unison like fiery, bright-orange comets.

About six minutes later, the huge, external fuel tank separated from the shuttle.

Discovery is bound for a construction mission on the International Space Stations (ISS), in orbit 400 kilometres above the Earth.

The launch was postponed Thursday due to cloud cover at the launch site. Prospects for a Saturday launch were initially poor, with severe crosswinds forecast, but the weather outlook improved throughout Saturday afternoon until the rescheduled blast off at 8:47 pm (0147 Saturday GMT).

With the successful launch, Discovery crew member Christer Fugelsang of Sweden becomes the first Scandinavian in space.

Discovery's crew of seven astronauts is scheduled to spend 12 days in orbit, mostly docked with the International Space Station.

The flight's primary mission is to hook up the ISS' permanent electricity generating system, replacing a temporary power system operating since the space station went into orbit in 1998. Two spacewalks are planned to rewire both halves of the station.

The plan is to deliver astronaut Sunita Williams to replace German astronaut Thomas Reiter after five and a half months in residence on the space station, which has a rotating crew of three astronauts.

Saturday's launch starts the second working shuttle mission to the ISS since NASA returned to flight in summer 2005, after the Shuttle Columbia disintegrated on re-entry in 2003.

NASA spent most of the last year testing new safety systems. A September flight by the Shuttle Atlantis marked the resumption of ISS construction.

The mission is the 33rd for Discovery and the 117th space shuttle launch since the programme began flying in 1981. With two catastrophic failures in its history, the shuttle fleet is slated to be mothballed after the scheduled completion of ISS construction in 2010.
