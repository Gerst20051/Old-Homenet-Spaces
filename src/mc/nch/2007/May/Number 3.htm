MySpace Cashes In on Widgets

Red Herring

News Corp.’s social site snaps up Photobucket for $300M. What’s next?
May 11, 2007

Rupert Murdoch is on the prowl again for that long-sought feather in his cap: Dow Jones, publisher of The Wall Street Journal. But he may have to settle for owning a piece of digital bling on MySpace pages for now.

 

Mr. Murdoch’s social network behemoth MySpace struck a deal this week to land No. 1 photo-sharing site Photobucket for as much as $300 million, a source familiar with the matter said. Fox Interactive Media, the News Corp. unit that runs MySpace, declined to comment. The deal is both strategic and defensive for Los Angeles-based MySpace because it will profit from Photobucket’s widget advertising, as well as stem the flood of users leaving the social network for the photo-sharing site. 

 

The acquisition allows MySpace to corral Palo Alto, California-based Photobucket’s 40 million users, not bad for a site that already has about 100 million monthly visitors. There is some overlap because nearly 59 percent of the photo-sharing site’s traffic comes from visitors to MySpace, according to Hitwise, but now MySpace should be able to hang on to most, if not all, of them.

 

The real key to the deal was control over Photobucket’s wildly popular--and profitable – widgets, pieces of embeddable software that show up as tiny windows on a user’s computer screen. Photobucket members have used widgets to post their photos on the social-networking site, but there was trouble last month when a flood of Photobucket video widgets containing advertising started showing up on MySpace. The social-networking site promptly blocked Photobucket video and photo widgets, and said it would do the same with other third-party widgets that sell or advertise without a revenue sharing agreement with MySpace.

 

Industry observers will now be closely watching Photobucket rivals RockYou, San Francisco-based Slide, and others to see what kinds of tolls MySpace could impose to do business on the social network.

 

Armies of widgets makers will also be watching to see how it all unfolds.  That’s because they offer an endless array of mini-applications that receive roughly 4 billion page views per day, Slide CEO Max Levchin told an audience at a recent Red Herring event. “The revenue opportunities are massive,” Mr. Levchin said.

 

But the challenge will be for social networks to reach revenue-sharing terms without alienating the vast pool of third-party widget makers who add headlines, videos, photos, stock tickers, and countless other features on the fly. For now, Mr. Slide said that until widget makers figure out how to “survive or make profits,” there’s no way social-networking companies can charge them.

 

VCs have a stake too, given that many widget makers are venture funded. So far, Photobucket investors made out well. Trinity Ventures led a $10.5 million second round investment in Photobucket in May 2006. If MySpace winds up paying $300 million, Trinity and other second-round investors could expect to see a return of about three to four times, according to a source familiar with the matter. “In less than a year—that is pretty good,” the source said. Under the deal, MySpace has agreed to pay $250 million plus an additional $50 million if certain performance targets are met by Photobucket, according to the source.

 

And Mr. Murdoch is in an acquisitive mood. His media empire has already bought

 a Turkish TV station, a software maker, a controlling stake in VeriSign’s Jamba and a targeted ad firm in the last nine months or so (Let’s not forget the $5 billion bid for Dow Jones). And News Corp still had $ 7.2 billion in cash at the end of its most recent quarter.

 

Now that could buy a lot of widgets.
