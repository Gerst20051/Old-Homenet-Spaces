Getting Copyright Off MySpace

Red Herring

Social network launches automated copyright filtration.
May 11, 2007

 

Social-networking site MySpace on Friday said it launched new software designed to prevent users from re-posting copyrighted videos after they had been taken down at the request of the content owner.

 

Several analysts and an industry representative whose company has a relationship with MySpace praised the site for being the first to implement automated filtering technology on such a large scale. But others pointed out that the heaviest burden still rests on the content owner to track the unauthorized use of copyrighted material and request its removal.

 

The move comes as media companies have expressed growing concern about the proliferation of copyrighted content on sites such as MySpace and video-sharing site YouTube. Media giant Viacom has taken the lead against Silicon Valley by filing a $1 billion copyright infringement suit against Google’s YouTube. Viacom has accused YouTube of being slow to add technology that would filter unauthorized content.

 

MySpace’s new feature relies on technology from Los Gatos, California-based startup Audible Magic that creates a digital “fingerprint” from audio clips of copyrighted material. The fingerprints will be added to a custom MySpace database, which will recognize and prevent unauthorized videos from being reposted to the site.

 

Several major media companies such as Viacom have already been working with Audible Magic to create digital fingerprints of their content. Audible Magic’s partnership with MySpace will now let other content owners search the site for copyrighted content, request to have it removed, and have fingerprints of that content added to the site’s database.

 

“Any kind of automated system that can judge ‘Is this copyrighted content or is this not copyrighted content,’ while minimizing false positives, is very hard to build,” said JupiterResearch analyst Joe Laszlo.

 

He added that one of the reasons MySpace may have taken a faster approach to dealing with the problem than, say, YouTube, is due to the fact that it is owned by News Corp., which has its own copyrights to protect.

 

While observers noted that MySpace has gone to greater lengths than competitors to police its site for unauthorized content, industry experts said the current Digital Millennium Copyright Act still makes it too difficult for content owners to protect their copyrighted material. The law requires copyright holders that want their content removed from video-sharing sites to scan the Internet for unauthorized use and then fire off take-down notices to the websites where the content is hosted.

 

An industry insider said the company has full-time staff devoted to the problem of online copyright infringement and sends YouTube alone hundreds of takedown notices each month.

 

MySpace said it did not have plans to filter all content for copyright infringement because some media groups want their content to reach as many people as possible. But MySpace said the automated system will make it easier to find and take down unauthorized videos. “That feels to me like maybe a compromise that both sides will be able to live with,” said Mr. Laszlo.

 

Gartner analyst Allan Wiener predicted huge growth in audio and video copyright-tracking technology through the rest of the year. He said London-based Red Bee Media and San Francisco’s Autonomy are two of a growing number of content tracking companies that stand to benefit from this trend.

 

“In the second half of this year, this is going to be a very, very hot topic,” Mr. Wiener said.

 

Audible Magic’s Mr. Ikezoye said his company will launch a video image fingerprinting technology that is currently being tested on live sites in the next few months. He would not say whether it is being tested on MySpace. Mr. Wiener added that the growth potential for such technologies will also be a big draw for venture capital.
