Poetry Poems

Just as poets use a variety of techniques to express themselves, their written poetry can fall into one or more categories. Three of these categories are described below.

LYRIC POETRY

    More than two thousand years ago in Greece, short poems called lyrikos were sung to the accompaniment of a lyre, a stringed musical instrument. Even when the poems were no longer sung, the name lyric remained.

    Lyric poetry is poetry whose sole purpose is to express the poet's personal feelings, attitudes, or reactions. The subject of the poem may be anything about witch the poet has strong feelings. Subjects may include emotions, a scene, an idea, a person, or an event. A lyric poem will often appeal to the readers' senses through figurative language and imagery. Lyric poems may take any form.

NARRATIVE POETRY

    Early narrative poems grew out of the need for entertainment (song) and for a means of "recording" and communicating important events or occurrences. Before witting and printing became common, narrative poems were often composed and sung by ordinary, uneducated people.

    Narrative poetry is poetry that tells a story. The story may be long and complicated, with many characters involved in numerous events, or it may be brief, with one character in conflict with another or with a situation. Narrative poems are similar to short stories in that they have a setting, plot, and characters, and usually have dialogue. Narrative poems may be rhymed or unrhymed and may be organized in a variety of forms.

DRAMATIC POETRY

    Dramatic poetry is poetry in which one or more characters speak to others who may or may not answer. The dramatic monologue is a type of dramatic poetry in which a speaker talks to a listener who does not speak. In dramatic poetry, the speaker generally reveals more about him or herself than in narrative poetry. A dramatic poem may be serious or humorous in nature and may take any form.
